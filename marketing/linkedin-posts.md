## Post 1 — Cheap Developer Story
**Format:** Long story | **Post:** Tuesday 8am EST

A restaurant owner called me two years ago.

She had paid $800 on Fiverr for a website. It looked okay on his screen. On mobile, half the menu was cut off. The contact form silently discarded every submission. She found out six months later when a regular customer asked why she never responded to his catering inquiry.

Six months of leads. Gone.

The developer wasn't a scammer. He probably did his best. But $800 gets you someone learning on your project. Someone who moves on the moment you stop watching. Someone who has no stake in whether your business actually grows.

I've had this conversation probably thirty times since starting ElevenClicks in 2019. The pattern is always the same: owner wants a website, finds someone cheap, gets something delivered, assumes it works, discovers months later it didn't.

By then they've paid twice. Once for the original work. Once to fix it.

If you're shopping for a developer right now, here's the one question to ask before signing anything:

"Can I talk to two past clients who are in the same industry as me?"

Not a portfolio link. A real conversation with a real business owner.

If the answer is no, that's your answer.

## Post 2 — Website Gets No Leads
**Format:** Medium tip | **Post:** Wednesday 12pm EST

Your website might be getting visitors and still generating zero leads.

Most small business websites are built to look good in a demo. But the moment a real visitor lands with a real problem, nothing guides them anywhere.

No clear headline that says what you do. A contact form buried three clicks deep. Phone number in tiny grey text in the footer. A "Learn More" button that goes to an About page instead of a booking link.

Visitors don't work hard to figure out how to hire you. They leave.

I reviewed a law firm's website last year. They were paying $300/month in Google Ads and getting maybe one call a week. We changed exactly three things: moved the phone number to the top of every page, replaced the hero headline with something specific to their practice area, and added a "Free 15-minute call" button above the fold.

Calls went to twelve a week. Same ad spend. Same traffic.

The site wasn't broken. It just wasn't doing the one job a website actually has.

If you're running ads or SEO right now, spend 20 minutes clicking through your own site as if you were a stranger who found you on Google. Does it tell you clearly what to do next?

## Post 4 — Don't Show Up on Google
**Format:** Medium expertise | **Post:** Wednesday 8am EST

I asked a clinic owner to Google her own business last year.

She typed in the exact service she offers, in the city she operates in.

She was on page four. Her competitor, who opened eight months after her, was listing two. Better location, fewer staff, nobody in her orbit had heard of them.

The difference was not ad spend. It was one thing: Google Business Profile, set up properly. Real photos, accurate hours, a clear description, recent reviews with responses.

That's it.

A huge number of Canadian small businesses either have no Google Business Profile or have one they set up in 2019 and never touched again. Wrong address. Old photos. No responses to reviews.

Google treats that as a signal that you're not active. And ranks you accordingly.

If you haven't logged into your Google Business Profile in the last six months, do it today. Update one thing. Respond to your last review. Add a recent photo.

Fifteen minutes. One of the highest-return things a local business owner can do.

And no, you don't need to pay anyone to do this part.

## Post 6 — Is Your Site Hurting You?
**Format:** Short punchy | **Post:** Friday 8am EST

Pull up your website on your phone right now.

Does it load in under three seconds? Does the text fit without zooming? Is there one obvious thing to tap to contact you?

If any of those is a no, your website is not neutral. It's actively sending people away.

More than 60% of local business website visits happen on mobile. A site that works on desktop and breaks on a phone isn't a website. It's a bad first impression at scale.

## Post 9 — Fixed-Price Myth
**Format:** Medium opinion | **Post:** Wednesday 8am EST

Fixed-price projects get a bad reputation they don't deserve.

The knock is that developers cut corners to protect their margin. Sometimes true. But hourly billing has its own problem: you have no idea what you're paying, and the incentive structure rewards slow work.

We've done fixed-price projects since 2019. Here's how it works:

The scope conversation happens before any contract is signed. We push back on vague requests. "A website with all the features" is not a scope. "A five-page site with a booking form, menu PDF, and Instagram feed" is.

When scope is clear, fixed price protects both sides. You know what you're paying. We know what we're building. Nobody's incentivized to drag it out or cut corners.

The horror stories about fixed-price projects are almost always about unclear scope, not the pricing model itself.

If a developer quotes you a fixed price without asking detailed questions first, that's the red flag. Not the fixed price itself.
